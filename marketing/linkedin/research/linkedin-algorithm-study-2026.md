Not all interactions on 
LinkedIn look the same
And not all of them are visible.
When someone likes or comments on your post, you see 
it. But when someone clicks through a carousel, watches 
a video, or follows a link, that also counts as an interaction. 
LinkedIn tracks it, the algorithm weighs it, but nobody 
walking past your post would ever know it happened.
All these clicks are invisible interactions, and they 
matter. A lot.
The Like button has been the default way to measure 
content performance since Facebook introduced it in 
2009 . That’s almost 20 years ago. Social media looked 
completely different back then; the apps, the formats, 
the reasons people logged in. A 13-slide carousel didn’t 
exist, and swiping through one certainly didn’t count as 
engagement. But it does now, because it is engagement, 
whether or not anyone pressed a button.
LinkedIn has caught up to that reality, at least for Company 
Pages, and clicks are factored into engagement 
calculations.

Is LinkedIn penalizing you for sharing links?
Links hurting your content performance 
is one of social media’s most spread 
myths. And while we can’t completely 
debunk it, our data says it’s not the whole 
story ( and what better platform to test it 
than the one with “link” in the name).
For Company Pages, it seems not to 
have a direct negative effect; in fact, 
our sample shows that posts with links 
get 50% more impressions and 40% 
more interactions. On Personal Profiles, 
the effect flips, and both impressions 
and interactions drop 27% and 20%, 
respectively. 
Our conclusion is that a link won’t, of 
course, save a weak post, and it won’t 
sink a strong one either. If you’re going 
to share a link, make sure the post around 
it is worth stopping for; relevant, useful, 
and not trying too hard to sell something. 
The link is just the door, you still have to 
make people want to walk through it.

Personal Profiles outperform Company Pages on impressions, interactions, and 
comments at almost every account size. But Company Pages get shared significantly 
more across every tier, which means they’re still essential for amplifying content. The 
most effective LinkedIn strategy isn’t choosing one over the other, but using your 
Personal Profile to start conversations and your Company Page to spread them.

Sample 
All figures are averages per post, unless otherwise stated. Outliers were not removed, 
but accounts are segmented by follower size throughout the study. This means that 
accounts with millions of followers going viral won’t skew the data for accounts with a 
few thousand followers. Each tier is analyzed independently.
Accounts with zero publications were excluded from the sample, as were individual posts 
with zero impressions or zero interactions.
Sample period 
The study data covers January–February of 2025 and 2026. 
Growth 
When we refer to growth, we mean that an account has gained enough followers to move 
up from one size tier to the next. An account that gained followers but stayed within 
the same tier is not counted as having grown in this context, not because it didn’t make 
progress, but because the threshold wasn’t crossed. This is worth keeping in mind when 
comparing growth rates across tiers, since moving from Tiny to Small requires fewer new 
followers than moving from Medium to Big.
Best posting times 
Best posting times are calculated based on the activity patterns of LinkedIn accounts 
connected to Metricool. Higher activity means more users are online and engaging 
with content during that window, increasing the likelihood your post will be seen. These 
times reflect when your audience is most active on the platform, not a guarantee of 
performance; results may vary depending on your specific audience and industry.
Metrics definitions
Impressions - The number of times your post was shown on LinkedIn. This number is an 
estimate and may not be precise.
Members reached - The number of distinct members and Pages that saw your post. This 
number is an estimate and does not include repeat displays.
Clicks - Shows the number of clicks on your content, company name, or logo by 
a signed-in member. This doesn’t include interactions like reposts, reactions, or 
comments. If a member clicks on a document multiple times, it will count as one click.
Clicks affect both engagement rate and interactions. When analyzing Company Pages in 
isolation, clicks are included in both metrics. When analyzing Personal Profiles in isolation, 
clicks are not included since LinkedIn does not track them the same way for personal 
accounts. Whenever we compare Company Pages and Personal Profiles directly, 
clicks are removed from the Company Page side to normalize the data and make the 
comparison fair and meaningful.
Interactions (Reactions, Comments, and Reposts)- Shows the number of reactions, 
comments, and times your content was shared by members, including Direct Sponsored 
Content and video posts.
Engagement rate - Shows the ratio of interactions per impressions on a post. 
Interactions include, reactions, comments, shares, and clicks (for Company Pages only).
