Today Score — circle compositions

Two valid directions for displaying the four pillars (Train · Nourish · Move · Sleep). Group A = one unified circular object representing all four pillars at once (most compact, most ownable, most different from Oura/Whoop). Group B = four distinct circles, one per pillar, in creative arrangements (matches the 2026 industry convention more closely, most legible per-pillar). Each variant is rendered at three fill states so empty-state legibility is visible. Pick one (or a hybrid).

Group A · Unified single composition

One circular object encodes all four pillars. Most ownable; lowest competitor overlap.

1. Quadrant ring

One unified ring split into four 90° arc-segments. Each arc fills around its quadrant from a fixed start angle. Center holds the overall day %.
Strengths: cleanest glance read, distinctive vs Oura/Whoop (they use 3 separate rings, not 4 segments of one), legible at small sizes.

2. Concentric tree rings

Four nested rings of increasing radius. Inner ring = Train, outer = Sleep. Each ring fills clockwise from 12 o'clock. Organic, layered metaphor (cross-section of a healthy day).
Strengths: completely novel for the category. Risk: inner rings are smaller, may need bolder strokes.

3. Pulse-orbit dial

Single outline ring. Four colored satellite dots travel its perimeter; each dot's angular position = its pillar progress (0% at 12 o'clock, 100% back at 12). Cinematic, motion-driven.
Strengths: dynamic, ownable. Risk: glance-reading exact % is harder.

4. Iris aperture

Four curved blade-arcs forming a closing iris (like a camera aperture). Blades extend toward the center as progress rises; pupil shrinks as the day fills. Empty = wide-open eye, full = closed eye.
Strengths: visceral metaphor ("the day closes in on its goal"). Risk: blades overlap → individual % can be ambiguous.

5. Half-moon stack

Four half-rings (180° each) nested concentrically with offset start angles — top, right, bottom, left. Creates an interlocking crescent pattern that turns into a flower when all four fill.
Strengths: distinctive silhouette. Risk: asymmetric, slightly busier than a clean ring.

6. Solar corona

Central filled disc + four colored arcs floating at increasing radii like a sun's halo. Each arc's angular length = its pillar progress. Reads as one luminous celestial object.
Strengths: warm, ownable. Risk: larger footprint than a single ring.

7. Wheel of fortune (radial pie)

Four fixed 90° wedges that fill radially outward from the center instead of around their arc. Empty day = small pinwheel; full day = full pie. Each wedge's radius = its pillar progress.
Strengths: progress visible at-glance via geometry, not just color. Risk: feels more "gauge" than "ring".

8. Pulse rings (sonar)

Central monogram + four concentric rippling rings. Each ring's saturation + amplitude = its pillar progress, gently pulsing like sonar.
Strengths: alive, soothing. Risk: motion may be distracting in a list.

Group B · Multi-circle (one per pillar)

Heads up: every Group B variant except B1 (and B12) makes the score card taller than the current home-screen slot. If preserving the existing compact footprint matters (it should — above-fold space is precious), pick from Group C below or from B1/B12.

Four distinct circles, each owning one pillar. Matches the 2026 industry convention (Oura/Whoop/Bevel/Google Health all use multi-ring), but each variant arranges them in a way Zealova can own. Rings now load-animate on scroll.

B1. Four peer rings — row

Four rings in a horizontal row. Each ring shows: a centered pillar glyph (barbell / leaf / shoe / moon), the % below the ring, and a hint line below that. Distinct from Oura/Whoop by replacing the bare center number with a meaningful glyph + by adding a faint inner companion ring for visual density.
Strengths: maximum per-pillar legibility; matches the 2026 convention with a Zealova twist.

B2. Diamond quartet

Four rings arranged in a diamond (top / right / bottom / left), forming a single visual cluster. A faint connecting outline links them. Train sits at top as the priority pillar; Sleep at bottom; Nourish and Move on the sides.
Strengths: reads as one shape from a distance; per-pillar at close range.

B3. Constellation

Four rings of slightly varying sizes scattered like a constellation, connected by thin dotted lines. The arrangement is deliberate (not random) so it's always the same shape — recognisable as a Zealova mark over time.
Strengths: brand-distinct; memorable. Risk: spatial layout is less rigid; needs careful tuning.

B4. Satellites

Central monogram disc with four pillar rings orbiting it at 12/3/6/9 o'clock positions. Visually centred, compact, and the central Z anchors the brand.
Strengths: tight footprint; gravitational read.

B5. Asymmetric weighted stack

Four rings sized by their base weight in the Today score: Train (40%, biggest), Nourish (30%), Move (15%), Sleep (15%). Hierarchy is visible in the geometry itself — the app's priorities are legible at a glance.
Strengths: communicates "what matters most today" without text. Risk: visually unbalanced; users may misread bigger = more done.

B6. Interlocking chain

Four rings interlocked in a 2×2 cluster (think Olympic rings but tighter). Each ring is independently fillable. The interlock metaphor reinforces "these four pillars are linked — slack one and the others suffer".
Strengths: meaningful metaphor; visually rich.

B7. Bubble cluster

Four rings clustered organically with soft drop shadows — like bubbles floating together. Each gently bobs vertically out of phase. Friendly, soft, low-pressure feel.
Strengths: emotional warmth. Risk: less rigorous-looking than the others.

B8. Card fan

Four rings on stacked, slightly rotated "cards" — like a fanned hand of playing cards. Train is the topmost card. Each card lifts slightly on hover.
Strengths: tactile, gamified feel. Risk: extra chrome may compete with the rings themselves.

B9. Honeycomb

Four rings tightly packed in a hex arrangement (one on top, two middle, one bottom). Reads as a single dense cell cluster.
Strengths: compact; mathematical. Works for widget sizes.

B10. Vertical timeline

Four rings stacked vertically with a thin spine connecting them, plus a small progress bar to the right of each ring. Reads like a checklist made of circles.
Strengths: highest information density. Risk: card takes more vertical space.

B11. Hero + trio

One large ring (Train, the priority pillar) anchors the left; three smaller satellite rings (Nourish · Move · Sleep) stack vertically on the right. Explicit hierarchy: training is the boss metric.
Strengths: storytelling layout — "this is the headline, these are the supporting metrics". Matches Zealova's workout-first identity.

B12. Linked horizontal chain

Four rings in a row joined by short colored connector segments that change color as adjacent pillars fill. When all four are full, the whole chain becomes one continuous gradient bar.
Strengths: rewarding "they all connect" moment at 100%. Playful + meaningful.

B13. Triptych + keystone

Three rings in a row (Train · Nourish · Move) with Sleep as a slightly elevated "keystone" centered above — like an architectural arch. Sleep crowns the day, the other three are the foundation.
Strengths: distinctive shape; sleep gets visual prominence without dominating.

B14. Gauge cluster (clock faces)

Four small ring-gauges in a 2×2 grid, each rendered like a tachometer / clock face — tick marks around the rim, a small colored needle indicating progress. Mechanical, dashboard-like.
Strengths: precise, "instrument panel" aesthetic. Risk: can feel cold or technical.

Group C · Compact horizontal row variants

All Group C variants preserve the current short-height horizontal score-card footprint — 4 pillars in a row, ~120–140pt tall. The only thing that varies is the per-pillar visual treatment. This is the bucket to pick from if you don't want to push the home-screen layout taller.

C1. Minimal numerical (Oura-shaped baseline)

Standard stroke-ring with a centered percentage. No glyph, no extra chrome. The "safe" choice that the entire industry uses. Included as a baseline to compare every other variant against.
Strengths: zero learning curve. Risk: indistinguishable from Oura/Whoop.

C2. Concentric companion (today + yesterday)

Outer ring = today's progress. Faint inner ring = yesterday's value, for context. Glyph at center. Adds information density without breaking the row layout.
Strengths: lets users see progress vs. yesterday at a glance, which is what Whoop/Oura users actually want.

C3. Liquid-fill circle ★

Each pillar is a circle (no stroke ring) that fills bottom-up with its accent color like water rising in a glass. The fill has a subtle wavy meniscus at the top that gently bobs. Empty state shows a faint outline + glyph.
Strengths: unique — nobody in the category does this. Visceral "fill up your day" metaphor. Same footprint as B1.

C4. Half-arc top + text bed

Each pillar is a 180° top arc (semicircle gauge) with the percentage + label tucked into the flat bottom area. Frees vertical space and lets the label sit inside the arc's "bed".
Strengths: most vertical-space-efficient of the row variants. Distinct semicircle silhouette.

C5. Dot-cluster ring (segmented)

Instead of a continuous stroke, each ring is made of 12 dots around the perimeter. Dots light up sequentially as progress fills. Gamified, pixel-art feel.
Strengths: rewarding micro-progress (each dot is a milestone). Playful.

C6. Ring + 7-day sparkline

Each ring sits above a tiny 7-day sparkline showing the pillar's trend. Adds longitudinal context without leaving the home screen.
Strengths: highest information density of any row variant. Whoop-graphics-card energy but in a tile-sized footprint.

C7. Soft-glow ring (atmospheric)

Standard stroke ring with a soft outer glow whose intensity matches progress. Filled pillars feel "alive"; empty ones recede. Atmospheric, warm.
Strengths: emotional pull — completed pillars literally glow. Same shape as Oura but visually richer.

C8. Squircle tokens (Apple-style)

Rings replaced with rounded-square (squircle) tokens, each holding a glyph + colored progress arc along the top edge. Same row footprint, very Apple/iOS native feel.
Strengths: familiar to iOS users; clean. Risk: less ownable.

C9. Liquid-fill + 7-day sparkline ★ (C3 × C6)

Liquid-fill circle on top + 7-day weekly trend sparkline below with M T W T F S S day labels (today bold + colored). Combines C3's unique fill metaphor with C6's longitudinal context.
Strengths: tells "today + this week's trajectory" in one tile. Best of both your favorites.

C10. Liquid-fill inside companion ring (C3 × C2)

Outer dashed ring carries a small tick mark at yesterday's level. Inside the ring, liquid fills bottom-up to today's level. You see today vs yesterday as the relationship between the liquid line and the tick mark.
Strengths: today + yesterday comparison built into the fill metaphor.

C11. Companion ring + sparkline (C2 × C6)

Outer ring = today. Faint inner ring = yesterday. Tiny weekly sparkline tucked below. Three time horizons in one tile: today, yesterday, last 7 days.
Strengths: maximum information density of any row variant. Risk: busiest visual.

C12. Liquid-fill + goal threshold line

Liquid-fill circle with a thin horizontal "goal" line drawn across at 80% — the threshold for a "win" day. When liquid rises above the line, the fill color saturates and a tiny ✓ appears. Clear "are you above goal?" read.
Strengths: explicit goal feedback. Visceral.

C13. Tide chart (meniscus = weekly trend)

Liquid-fill circle where the meniscus wave at the top of the liquid is actually a 7-day mini chart — each peak/trough = one day's completion height. Most differentiated visual in the file; risk is novelty cost.
Strengths: signature visual. Risk: takes a second to understand the wave is data.

C14. Calendar week-dot ring

Like C5 dot-cluster but each of 7 dots around the ring represents a day of this week. Each dot's saturation = that day's completion. Today's dot is bigger and pulses. Center holds today's %.
Strengths: weekly streak visible in the ring itself.

C15. Liquid-fill + delta chip (today vs yesterday)

Liquid-fill circle + a small pill chip floating bottom-right showing "+12%" or "−5%" change vs yesterday with an arrow. Lighter than a sparkline; more focused than a companion ring.
Strengths: at-glance change number is the most actionable single bit of info.

Group G · Creative ring treatments (compact row, % inside)

Inspired by your Whoop / Bevel reference — these go beyond the bare stroke ring with open-arc shapes, gradient sweeps, halo glows, beveled depth, and other visual textures. All keep the same compact horizontal row footprint, and all keep the % inside the ring as you requested.

G1. Open-arc gradient (Whoop-inspired) ★

270° arc with a gap at the top, stroke sweeps from light to vivid color around the arc, soft halo behind. The arc shape itself is distinctive — not just a closed ring.

G2. Neon glow ring

Vibrant solid stroke with a multi-layer outer glow. Filled pillars genuinely "glow"; empty ones recede. Works beautifully in dark mode.

G3. Beveled 3D ring

Ring with depth — inner shadow + outer highlight creates a pressed-in / engraved feel. Tactile, premium, not flat.

G4. Spiral progress (1.5 turns)

Instead of a single ring, the progress draws as a 1.5-turn spiral going outward. Unusual, motion-friendly, signature visual.

G5. Liquid glass orb (3D)

A glassy 3D sphere with internal liquid fill rising bottom-up, plus highlights and reflections. The premium fit of C3 with depth + light.

G6. Aurora gradient sweep

Ring stroke uses a 4-stop gradient (light → mid → vivid → dark) sweeping around the arc, like an aurora. Most color-rich variant.

G7. Notched bezel (watch face)

Ring surrounded by tick marks like a watch bezel; major ticks every 25%. Precision-instrument aesthetic.

G8. Spark ring

Ring made of small sparks/particles around the perimeter that light up sequentially with progress. Filled = bright cluster of sparks; empty = dim dots.

C2 fine-tunes · center content + comparator decisions

Six versions of the same compact-row ring layout, varying only what sits in the center and whether to keep an inner comparator ring. All at the partial-day fill state for direct comparison. Pick one to lock the final treatment.

F1. % in center, no comparator

Classic Oura-shape. Reads "67% complete" — chore-flavored.

F2. Number in center (no %), pillar name below ★

Number reads as a score (positive framing). Pillar name below. Cleanest. Matches Oura/Whoop convention.

F3. Glyph in center, number below

Pillar identity is the centerpiece (icon-first). Number tucked below. More iconic, slightly less data-forward.

F4. Number in center + faint background glyph

Best of both: bold number forward, glyph as a 0.15-opacity watermark behind it for identity without competing.

F5. Number + tiny delta text under pillar name

No inner ring, but small text comparator: "+12% vs yesterday" under the pillar label. Comparator without visual complexity. Question: does this duplicate the coach hero's job?

F6. Inner ring = 7-day average (not yesterday)

If you want to keep an inner comparator ring, 7-day average is more useful than yesterday — smooths variance, answers "am I above/below my typical". Reads as "today vs your normal".

Group D · Trend-first / full-card sparkline variants

What if the trend is the headline, not the snapshot? These variants make the sparkline (or its equivalent) the dominant element. Bigger sparkline area, more legible day labels, more honest week-at-a-glance. Some demote today's snapshot to a small chip; some combine both.

🔍
What 2026 user research says
Whoop's redesign research → users wanted coaching insights, not more chart types. Oura's 2025 redesign pivoted to surfacing one metric the body needs most right now. The dominant 2026 finding is that actionable intelligence beats raw data viz. Practical implication: today-snapshot wins above the fold, the AI coach hero matters more than ring shape, and trends belong on a tap-through screen — not hogging the home card.

D1. Stacked sparklines — full card

Four horizontal sparkline rows, one per pillar. Each shows the 7-day trend at full card width with M T W T F S S labels, today highlighted. No rings at all — the trend IS the card. Today's % sits on the right.
Strengths: most readable trend in the file. Risk: loses the at-glance "where am I today" hit.

D2. Area sparklines + mini-ring chip

Same as D1 but each sparkline is filled (area chart) and gets a small ring chip on the left showing today's %. Compromise between snapshot and trend.
Strengths: both reads in one card. Whoop's graphics-card energy in tile size.

D3. Multi-line overlay (all 4 in one chart)

All four pillars plotted as colored lines on a single chart. Today's points highlighted. Reads the whole week as one image.
Strengths: shows pillar correlations (sleep down → train down?). Risk: line cross-over can get visually noisy; hard to glance-read a single pillar.

D4. Grouped bars (7 days × 4 pillars)

Each day gets a cluster of 4 short bars (one per pillar). Compact way to see "how was each day across all four pillars".
Strengths: each day visible. Risk: tiny bars at this size; harder to read trend per pillar.

D5. Heatmap (4 rows × 7 days)

Each row = one pillar. Each cell = one day. Color saturation = that day's completion. Today's column outlined. Reads like a GitHub contribution chart.
Strengths: most info-dense; pattern-spotting at a glance ("Mondays I always skip Move"). Risk: feels analytical / less emotional.

D6. Stream / ribbon stack

Four colored ribbons stacked vertically; each ribbon's thickness across the week = completion height. Reads as a flowing organic stream — the whole week's shape at once.
Strengths: aesthetic, unique. Risk: per-pillar % requires inspection.

D7. Hybrid: snapshot row + trend strip ★ (best of both)

Top half of the card = compact row of 4 liquid-fill circles (C3-style snapshot). Bottom half = a single shared "stream" sparkline strip showing this week's overall day-score trend. Both reads, single card, still compact.
Strengths: matches Whoop's 3-dial + insight pattern, adds a week trend without taking 4 separate sparkline rows.
